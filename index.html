Images and visual media

I can't see images from pasted text — but if your content includes photos, illustrations, charts, or other visuals, licensing is one of the most commonly overlooked compliance questions in web publishing.

The most important thing to understand: the absence of copyright information on an image does not mean it is free to use. In the U.S., copyright attaches automatically at the moment of creation. No copyright notice is required. An image without visible attribution is not an unprotected one.

Does this content include images, photos, charts, or other visual media?

For each image in your content, you need to be able to answer three questions: who owns it, what license covers it, and does that license permit this specific use.

Stock photography — images from services like Adobe Stock, Getty, or Shutterstock are licensed, not purchased. That license specifies exactly what you can do with the image. Editorial licenses permit use in news and commentary contexts but not in promotional or commercial ones. A license obtained for one publication does not automatically extend to a new site with a different audience and purpose.

Creative Commons images — images with a CC license are free to use under specific conditions. But the conditions vary significantly by license type. CC BY requires attribution. CC BY-NC prohibits commercial use. CC BY-ND prohibits modification. CC BY-NC-ND prohibits both. Reading the specific license before using the image is not optional.

Images found via Google — appearing in search results does not indicate licensing status. Use Google's image search filter — Tools → Usage Rights — to find images with Creative Commons licenses. Better still, use a dedicated source like Unsplash, Pexels, or Wikimedia Commons where license information is explicit.

Images from published research — figures, charts, and diagrams from journal articles are copyrighted by the publisher unless published under an open access or Creative Commons license. Reproducing them without permission is infringement even if the research itself is publicly available.

Each one needs descriptive alt text — not a label, but a description of what the image communicates. A screen reader user should get the same information from the alt text that a sighted user gets from the image.

Does this content include video or audio?

Video needs captions. Audio needs a transcript. These are not optional enhancements — they are the primary way deaf and hard-of-hearing users access this content.

Does this content use color to convey information?

Color alone cannot be the only way information is communicated. Charts, graphs, and callouts that rely solely on color are inaccessible to colorblind users.
